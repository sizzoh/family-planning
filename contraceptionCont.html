Two types
  1. Combined oral contraceptive pills (COC)
  2. Progestin only contraceptive pills (POC)

Combined Oral Contraceptives

•Pills that contain low doses of 2 hormones-a progestin and an estrogen-like the natural hormones progesterone and estrogen in a woman's body.
• M/ A: prevent ovulation.
Effectiveness:
depends on the user: When no pill-taking mistakes are made, less than 1 pregnancy per 100 women using COCs over the first year (3 per 1,000 women)
advantages
    • Are control led by the woman
    • Can be stopped at any time without a provider's help
    • Do not interfere with sex
    • Are easy to use
    • Easy to obtain, for example, in drug shops or pharmacies
    • Reduce: Menstrual cramps, Menstrual bleeding problems, Ovulation pain
Disadvantages
    Side effects and health risks
    •Do not protect against STls
    • Needs everyday action
    • Effectiveness depends on user
    Known Health Risks
    • Very rare: Blood clot in deep veins of legs or lungs (deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism)
    • Extremely rare: Stroke, Heart attack

Who can use COCs
• Have or have not had children, married or are not married
• Are of any age, including adolescents and women over 40 years old
• After childbirth and during breastfeeding, after 6 months
• Have just had an abortion, miscarriage, or ectopic pregnancy
• Smoke cigarettes-if under 35 years old
• Have anemia now or had in the past
May not be suitable
•less than 6 weeks since giving birth even if not breastfeeding (risk of DVT)
• Primarily breastfeeding between 6 weeks and 6 months since giving birth
• Age 35 or older and smokes cigarettes
• High blood pressure
• History of jaundice while using COCs in the past
• Gallbladder disease ( current or medically treated)
• has migraine headaches
• Had breast cancer
• Diabetes
• Taking barbiturates, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, phenytoin, primidone, topiramate, rifampicin, or rifabutin. These medications reduce the effectiveness of COCs.
• Taking lamotrigine. COCs may make lamotrigine less effective.

When to Start
  • If it is more than 5 days after the start of her monthly bleeding, she can start COCs any time it is reasonably certain she is not pregnant.
  • She will need a backup method for the first 7 days of taking pills.
